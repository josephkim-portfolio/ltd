Competency 1

To provide students with experiences to develop theory-grounded and research-based competencies for the innovative, aesthetic, effective and sustainable design/development and management of technologies for learning opportunities and systems.

Artifact: Japanese Meal Etiquette

  • Course:  ISLT 7383 Rapid Development Tools for Online Learning
  • Artifact:  Screen images from an interactive module that I designed in Adobe Captivate 9. The interactive module teaches Japanese meal etiquette to first time English teachers in Japan.
  • Author:  Joseph Kim, November 2017

I designed and developed this learning module in Adobe Captivate as a project in the ISLT 7383 course, Rapid Development Tools for Online Learning.

Since the English language is popular in Japan, many English language centers hire foreigners to teach English. For non-natives in Japan, learning the many social customs of the country can be a daunting endeavor. This interactive training module aims to teach basic Japanese social customs for aspiring English language instructors preparing to live in Japan for the first time.

Objectives for this module include:

  • After reading and watching videos, the learner will be able to identify Japanese honorific differences in speech and physical gestures in relating with older, peer, and younger students.
  • Given video and audio examples, the learner will be able to recognize common greetings in the workplace, at an acquaintance's home, and at restaurants.

Assessments:

  • A scenario-based quiz where the learner assumes the role of a newly hired English teacher having a meal at a restaurant with the English institute’s staff.
  • Quiz questions entail multiple choice answers in various situations that unfold at the restaurant.

Reflections

Strengths include:

  • Usage of actual images within a Japanese restaurant
  • Usage of videos of native Japanese speakers
  • Usage of common Japanese meal etiquette scenarios
  • A scenario-based quiz assessment

Weaknesses include:

  • The learning module does not contain a detailed preview of learning objectives.
  • The learning module does not contain a menu of lessons.

The following entail reflections on how I would create this learning module differently. I would include a menu of lessons and accompanying learning objectives at the start of the learning module. This would allow learners to know what to expect as they start the module, and select to either progress through the entire module or to selectively focus on lessons they are interested in.

Competency 2

To provide students with experiences to develop skills in data collection, analysis and evaluation for reflecting on and applying to the design/development of learning opportunities and systems.

Artifact: Performance Analysis Worksheet

  • Course:  ISLT 9474 Needs Assessment for Learning and Performance
  • Artifact:  A Performance Analysis Worksheet
  • Author:  Joseph Kim, November 2016

I completed this Performance Analysis Worksheet following Dr. Rose Marra's template. It considers the registration department of a university’s graduate program. The registration department’s duties entail providing registration services for new and continuing students in our Online program. Each term is 10 weeks in duration, which can create a crammed registration experience for both the staff and for the students.

The worksheet focuses on the performance problem of students attaining Priority Registration status, which is the department’s category of students who have the privilege of an early registration date. If more returning students can obtain Priority Registration status, there will be increased satisfaction for both students and staff.

Reflections

Strengths include:

  • Detailed questions that aim to identify specific causes that led to a student’s attainment or unattainment of Priority Registration Status.

Weaknesses include:

  • The length of the survey may cause a lack of response from students.

How would I do it differently now?

Since a higher quantity of student survey responses would be more valuable than fewer survey responses, I would reduce the 10 question survey to be 3 key questions. By advertising the survey as a short 3 question survey, along with a reward incentive, such as a raffle or a prize for students who submit a survey, it may evoke a higher quantity of responses from students.

Competency 3

To provide students with experiences to become socially responsible, reflective/sensitive on the interaction of technologies & society and act ethically in response to current and future challenges of emerging technologies for learning.

Artifact: Customized OSCQR Rubric

  • Course:  ISLT 9480 Internship in Information Science & Learning Technologies
  • Artifact:  A rubric to assess an online course's quality.
  • Author:  Joseph Kim, July 2020

In my ISLT 9480 Internship course, I chose to develop a customized OSCQR Rubric to review online courses at Azusa Pacific University. This artifact involved drawing online course quality standards from two publicly available course quality rubrics, and tailoring the rubric to suit the online course quality review needs at Azusa Pacific University. The tailored rubric was designed to review the quality of the university’s Online Asynchronous and Online Synchronous courses. This proposal served a real need for Azusa Pacific University's new online course review process. The result kept OSCQR's five quality domains of: Syllabus, Course Design, Interaction, Assessment, and Accessibility. Certain standards were rephrased, re-worded, re-grouped, removed, or added, in collaboration with the Director of Online Learning.

The customized OSCQR Rubric meets Competency 3 by promoting quality improvement of online courses to program directors and instructors, thereby improving the future online student and instructor experiences in these courses. The instrument seeks to improve aspects of the online course’s syllabus, course design, interaction, assessments, and accessibility, which in turn should improve the experience of both students and instructors. The accessibility standards addressed by the rubric considers the experience of online students with disabilities, and promotes ADA compliance for a variety of course standards.

The customized rubric drew inspiration from two rubrics - the Online Learning Consortium’s OSCQR Course Design Review Rubric version 3.1, and the Online Education Initiative’s Course Design Rubric (April 2020 version). Both of these rubrics allowed use and modification under their Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International licensing.

Reflections

Strengths include:

The strength of the customized OSCQR rubric is that it draws from two nationally recognized online course quality rubrics, both of which have past and present implementation. The rubric builds on working rubrics vetted and used by faculty, program directors, and instructional designers. Standards found in the rubric’s Syllabus, Course Design, Interaction, and Assessment sections are adapted from the Online Learning Consortium's OSCQR Course Design Review Rubric version 3.1. Standards in the Accessibility section are adapted from both the Online Education Initiative's (OEI) Course Design Rubric, April 2020 version, and the OSCQR Course Design Review Rubric.

Weaknesses include:

  • The broad scope of standards included, which contribute to a time-consuming review process.
  • The review notes and recommendations are reviewed by an Instructional Designer only.
  • The rubric can be used by faculty, but successful use requires in-depth knowledge of the quality standards. Instructors can use the rubric to peer-review each other's online courses, but requires an understanding of the quality standards for accurate peer-reviews.

Materials Used:

  • Google Sheets
  • Online Learning Consortium's OSCQR Course Design Review Rubric version 3.1
  • Online Education Initiative's (OEI) Course Design Rubric, April 2020 version
